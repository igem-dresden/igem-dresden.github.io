iGEM 2022 at TU Dresden

What is iGEM?

The International Genetically Engineered Machine (iGEM) is a student competition dedicated to the advancement of synthetic biology, interdisciplinary cooperation and education.

Synthetic Biology

What is Synthetic Biology?

Synthetic biology is a field of science that involves redesigning organisms for useful purposes by engineering them to have new abilities. Synthetic biology researchers and companies around the world are harnessing the power of nature to solve problems in medicine, manufacturing and agriculture.

The Problem

Chronic Wounds

Chronic wounds are a growing problem and affect millions of patients each year in Germany alone. Worse yet, chronic wounds are susceptible to bacterial infections. Long-term usage of antibiotics is sometimes required and contributes to the emergence of antibiotic-resistant strains, which has been declared by the WHO as “a global health threat”.

Our Solution

Phage Therapy | Healing Factors | Hydrogel

We will produce bacteriophages to target antibiotic-resistant bacteria that infect wounds by adopting a phage therapy approach. Moreover, we will use yeast cells as biofactories for the synthesis of human growth factors which will be employed to stimulate the healing of chronic wounds. The bacteriophages and the human growth factors would be combined into a hydrogel dressing to administer them to the chronic wound tissue.

We also plan to employ mathematical and molecular dynamics in-silico approaches to simulate and model the interactions of the therapeutics inside the hydrogel polymer mesh and their release so as to gain insights into the effects of different hydrogel compositions on the diffusion processes inside the hydrogel.

Our Team

About Our Team

We are a team of students representing TU Dresden at the 2022 International Genetically Engineered Machine (iGEM) competition. Our team is composed of 13 members from 6 different countries - Italy, Belarus, USA, Lithuania, Russia, and Germany - with students from all three master programs of the Center for Molecular and Cellular Bioengineering (CMCB) and from Informatik, Medieninformatik, and the für Bildende Künste.

The interdisciplinarity of our team leads to fruitful discussions during meetings and in the lab and has already proved valuable to making headways and obtaining early results. We feel lucky we have had the great opportunity to meet through the excellent environment offered here at TU Dresden.
